Three Day Startup

My experience at the LAUNCH.ed entrepeneurship program

Posted on October 26, 2018

During the last weekend of October I attended LAUNCH.ed's 3 Day Startup event at the Holyrood Outreach center.

3 Day Startup’s mission is to activate entrepreneurial potential in students of all kinds through experiential education and a global entrepreneurship ecosystem.

It brought together 60 students from many backgrounds to work on interdisciplinary projects proposed by the attendees. We selected the best ideas, built the concept for a startup and then explored competition and consumer interest.

Once we had that, we built a business model and some prototypes, to later pitch the concept to judges at the end of the weekend.

It was a tremendously fast-paced and useful event, during which I learned some of the challenges involved in running a startup through hands on experience and the advice and challenges of mentors and experts.

This is my experience during the program!

Day One: Team Forming


Idea generation

The LAUNCH.Ed team gave a brief introduction, and we were quickly split into random groups. We were sent to a meeting room, told to pitch ideas to each other and then choose our favourite two. There were some really interesting ideas: an “Uber for printing”, a blog to promote cruelty free products…

We landed on two ideas - a safety wearable for skiers, and the idea I pitched - a platform where users could submit and download audioguides and walking tours around cities.


The elevator pitch

Each person that proposed their idea and had it selected pitched it to the whole audience for a minute, and 8 were chosen. My idea was that users could submit walking tours with audioguides around cities, for like $1 a download. As a platform, we’d take a percentage for every download, and that’s how we’d generate revenue.

We all voted for our favourite idea, the top 8 were selected as the final startups - and mine was chosen! SmarTrips was born, and I was team leader for the weekend. People approached the team leaders and asked about the weekend plans, they gave us a meeting room and we started planning!

Day Two: Our Lean Startup

We came in, had an info session about the customer discovery process, and had a few minutes to plan our strategy. We then split into pairs and ran out to the street to meet our customers!

Edinburgh is a really touristy city, and so finding potential customers was easy. I quickly learned that:

  • people either plan their trips very carefully, or do not plan at all.
  • tourists enjoy taking a bus tour on the first day to get a sense of what’s around.
  • never underestimate a physical guide book!

The lightning round

When we got back to the venue, we had lunch straight away, and then - the lightning round! I went back to my team’s meeting room, and we all worked on our startup for an hour following the lean canvas.

Every fifteen minutes, a new pair of mentors would come in, listen to our pitch, give us feedback and challenge our ideas with their experience and knowledge. This helped us mould the original idea into a startup, and simplify it so we could communicate our idea in a five minute pitch.

In retrospect, this is where I take the most from - all mentors provided really valuable advice, but not everyone had the same ideas. I took each mentor’s advice too literally, and that made me loose focus on the original idea. I learned that I should take the best out of every piece of feedback, but not get distracted from the core mission or change it drastically every fifteen minutes!

Pitching SmarTrips at the end of Day 2

Pitch perfect

After the lightning round, we had time to prepare our second pitch. We had five minutes to explain our plan, and then five minutes to answer questions. During the afternoon, I had split the team into smaller groups, focused on key areas: go to market strategy, user interface and experience and customer segmentation.

We didn’t have much time, so I gave each focus team one minute of the pitch, and then structured the presentation. it went really well! We kept it focused and simple, and finished in under four minutes.
